Summary & Strategic Communications

Indiana is experiencing its most severe surge in residential electric bills in at least two decades, with statewide average bills rising 17.5 percent — or about $28 per month — in the single year ending July 1, 2025, driven by a wave of approved rate hikes from Duke Energy, NIPSCO, and CenterPoint Energy. The Indiana Utility Regulatory Commission (IURC) has launched an unprecedented investigative inquiry into energy affordability, Governor Mike Braun has appointed a new consumer-focused Utility Consumer Counselor, and legislators passed landmark performance-based ratemaking reform (HEA 1002) in 2026, but consumer advocates warn that low-income households remain acutely vulnerable with minimal summer disconnection protections and LIHEAP funding far below demonstrated need. Major pending proceedings — including AES Indiana's contested $192.9 million rate request and Duke Energy's $3.3 billion coal-to-gas conversion — will shape ratepayer costs for years to come while the rapid growth of energy-intensive data centers adds pressure on the grid.

Recent Articles

Ratepayer advocates recommend energy bill 'relief plan'

Citizens Action Coalition and Indiana Conservation Voters jointly released a comprehensive ratepayer relief plan in December 2025 calling for low-income discount rates, a summer utility shutoff ban, caps on utility profit rates, and changes to regulatory oversight. The plan proposed tying the low-income discount threshold to a 3–4 percent income burden standard similar to the 30 percent standard used in housing affordability assessments. A Bellwether Research poll cited in the plan found approximately 87 percent of Hoosiers were at least somewhat concerned about rising energy costs.

Indiana Capital Chronicle · 2025-12-10
Duke's $3.3B coal-to-gas conversion, third-party coal study approved

The IURC approved Duke Energy Indiana's $3.3 billion plan to replace two coal-fired units at its aging Cayuga Generating Station with natural gas combined-cycle units, with the costs to be recovered through ratepayer-funded trackers beginning in 2026. Duke estimated the approach would save customers $812 million compared to a traditional rate case but consumer advocates including Citizens Action Coalition strongly opposed it, calling it a 'loss' for both ratepayers and the environment. The approval also included a provision allowing Duke to explore selling the retired coal units to a third party.

Indiana Capital Chronicle · 2025-10-30
Indiana to investigate utilities after lawmakers raise concerns over residents' bills

Following a letter from Indiana legislators citing a more-than-90-percent increase in NIPSCO electric bills since 2016, the Indiana Utility Regulatory Commission announced it would launch a formal investigative inquiry into energy affordability. The lawmakers' letter specifically flagged that NIPSCO has among the highest residential consumer rates of all electric utilities reported to the U.S. Energy Information Administration. The IURC's inquiry ultimately expanded into a full-day public proceeding held on March 24, 2026.

WFYI (Indiana Public Broadcasting) · 2025-10-01
State's ratepayer advocate recommends denial of AES' $200M proposed increase

Newly appointed OUCC Commissioner Abby Gray announced that her office found AES Indiana had 'not demonstrated sufficient evidence' for a rate increase and recommended not only denying the $192.9 million request but actually reducing current rates by $21.2 million. Gray highlighted over 6,800 written consumer comments — the most ever received in an individual rate case — reflecting widespread customer hardship. Citizens Action Coalition simultaneously filed testimony urging the IURC to deny the hike in its entirety.

Indiana Governor Mike Braun appointed Abby Gray as the new Office of Utility Consumer Counselor commissioner on September 3, 2025, directing her to evaluate utility profits and find cost-saving measures to ease the financial burden on Hoosiers. Braun stated he would like utility investors to bear more of the cost of doing business instead of passing increases entirely to ratepayers. The appointment came as AES Indiana's $192.9 million rate request was pending and electric bills across the state had hit their highest levels in two decades.

High utility prices nearing crisis, and there are multiple factors behind the rise

A detailed analysis found that Indiana's rising utility costs stem from multiple compounding factors, including infrastructure investment recovery trackers, rising fuel costs, data center load growth, and insufficient regulatory guardrails. The article noted that so-called 'trackers' allow utilities to adjust rates for specific costs outside the normal rate case process, contributing to frequent bill increases between formal rate cases. Reporters noted the situation had risen to be considered more urgent by some Statehouse observers than the ongoing property tax debate.

CAC Program Director Ben Inskeep said 2025 marked the highest year-over-year jump in electric bills in two decades, arguing that Indiana's 2023 law requiring the IURC to weigh affordability, reliability, resiliency, stability, and environmental impact is failing ratepayers. Inskeep noted NIPSCO customers were seeing nearly $50-a-month increases, and said the law's affordability provisions lack sufficient enforcement mechanisms. WFYI / Indiana Public Broadcasting · 2025-07-24
Hoosiers' electric bills spike 17.5% in one year

A Citizens Action Coalition analysis of the five investor-owned electric utilities found that Indiana residential bills rose an average of 17.5 percent — or $28 per month — in the 12-month period ending July 1, 2025, the steepest single-year jump in at least two decades. NIPSCO customers were hardest hit at a 26.7 percent increase ($50/month), followed by CenterPoint at 25 percent ($44/month). Consumer advocates called the trend a 'crisis of affordability' and demanded immediate regulatory and legislative action.
