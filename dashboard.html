Summary & Strategic Communications

Indiana is experiencing a significant energy affordability crisis, with all five major investor-owned utilities having sought or received rate increases in the past 12–18 months, prompting unprecedented legislative and regulatory action. Governor Braun signed HEA 1002 in February 2026 to restructure rate approvals and mandate low-income assistance programs, while the IURC launched a landmark Investigative Inquiry on Energy Affordability in March 2026 — the first of its kind in the state. Despite these reforms, consumer advocates and the governor himself have expressed disappointment that the IURC still approved a $71 million AES Indiana rate increase in June 2026, and the OUCC has accused Duke Energy of over-collecting approximately $90 million beyond what regulators authorized.

Messaging Recommendations Silverman & Smith

Low-income ratepayers and vulnerable households
You are not alone — Indiana law now protects you from summer shutoffs during extreme heat if you are enrolled in LIHEAP, and new utility assistance programs are required under HEA 1002; contact your local Community Action Agency today to apply for the Energy Assistance Program and learn your rights before disconnection threatens your safety.
Indiana legislators and Governor's office
Rate increases continue to outpace inflation and disproportionately burden residential customers over large commercial users — enact enforceable affordability standards tied to household income, expand LIHEAP enrollment outreach, and ensure the IURC has clear performance metrics and accountability mechanisms under HEA 1002 that translate into measurable bill relief.
General Indiana public and media
Indiana has gone from having some of the lowest electric bills in the nation to the 12th highest, with NIPSCO customers seeing approximately a 16.75% rate increase phased in beginning July 2025, Duke Energy customers facing a $295.7 million annual revenue increase, and AES Indiana customers now facing a new $71 million increase — the cumulative cost of these rate cases falls hardest on everyday Hoosier households.
IURC commissioners and regulatory community
Apply HEA 1002's performance-based ratemaking tools rigorously: use quarterly utility data on arrears, disconnects, and burden rates to hold utilities accountable, and treat affordability — not just infrastructure investment recovery — as a primary regulatory standard in every pending and future rate proceeding.

WFYI · 2026-06-10
Indiana now blocks utility shutoffs on the hottest days — but only for some customers

Under a new Indiana law (HEA 1002), privately and cooperatively owned electric utilities are prohibited from disconnecting customers within 48 hours of a forecasted heat index of 95 degrees or higher — but only if the customer is enrolled in the federal LIHEAP energy assistance program. Consumer advocates noted the protection is narrower than proposed, leaving many low-income households that haven't enrolled in LIHEAP without coverage.

Gov. Braun says new law will protect Indiana utility customers from rising bills

Governor Mike Braun ceremonially signed HEA 1002 at the Statehouse on April 6, 2026, calling it landmark legislation to overhaul utility regulation and put affordability first. The law requires performance-based multi-year rate plans, mandates low-income customer assistance programs, and adds summer disconnection protections tied to heat index forecasts.
