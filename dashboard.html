Summary & Strategic Communications

Indiana is experiencing its most severe residential electric bill increases in at least two decades, with a statewide average jump of 17.5% (roughly $28/month) between July 2024 and July 2025 — nearly double the prior year's record. Three of Indiana's five major investor-owned utilities (Duke Energy Indiana, NIPSCO, and CenterPoint Energy) sought rate increases exceeding $40/month in 2024-2025, while AES Indiana filed a fresh $192.9 million rate request in June 2025, even as NIPSCO customers now face the state's highest electric bills at $234/month average. The affordability crisis is compounded by threatened federal LIHEAP funding cuts, the elimination of LIHEAP program staff, and a growing wave of data-center-driven infrastructure costs that advocates warn could be shifted to residential ratepayers.

Recent Articles

AES customers file opposition to rate hike settlement

The OUCC and Citizens Action Coalition filed formal opposition testimony against the AES Indiana partial settlement agreement, joined by more than 180 pages of consumer comments stating they cannot afford additional rate increases. The settlement — reached between AES, the City of Indianapolis, Walmart, Rolls-Royce, and major industrial customers — would result in a smaller residential rate increase than originally requested but still drew opposition from the state's ratepayer advocate and consumer groups who argued it was an unfair backroom deal that left residential customers exposed.

Axios Indianapolis · 2025-12-15
Ratepayer advocates recommend energy bill 'relief plan'

Indiana Conservation Voters and the Citizens Action Coalition jointly released a policy plan calling for income-based utility bill discounts (capped at a 3-4% income burden threshold), a summertime disconnection ban for low-income customers, performance-based regulation reforms, and restrictions on cost-shifting from large data-center customers to residential ratepayers. A Bellwether Research poll commissioned for the release found that approximately 87% of Hoosiers were at least 'somewhat' concerned about rising energy costs, with 59% 'very' concerned. The plan was timed ahead of the 2026 legislative session.

Indiana Capital Chronicle · 2025-12-10
AES rate hike case heats up as opposition builds

Opposition to AES Indiana's $192.9 million rate hike request intensified following the OUCC's recommendation that the IURC deny the increase entirely and instead reduce current rates by approximately $21 million. The Indianapolis City-County Council joined bipartisan opposition, citing both the rate hike and ongoing billing system problems tied to a 2023 software upgrade. Consumer advocates noted that nearly half of AES customers in Indianapolis received a disconnection notice in the first six months of 2025.

State agency recommends denial of AES Indiana's rate increase

The Indiana OUCC filed testimony recommending the IURC deny AES Indiana's $192.9 million base rate increase request, arguing that AES had not demonstrated sufficient evidence for a hike filed less than 14 months after its previous rate case was settled. The OUCC's filing included more than 6,800 written comments from AES customers and other stakeholders, along with accounts from 89 customers who spoke at four public hearings. The OUCC recommended the IURC instead reduce AES's current rates by roughly $21 million.

WTHR (NBC Indianapolis) · 2025-09-03
High utility prices nearing crisis, and there are multiple factors behind the rise

Indiana Capital Chronicle reporter Niki Kelly reviewed the structural drivers of Indiana's utility cost surge, including tracker mechanisms that allow utilities to recover specific infrastructure costs outside of formal rate cases, growing data center electricity demand, and a regulatory rate-game in which utilities propose inflated hikes expecting to be negotiated down. The piece noted that so-called trackers now make up as much as 13.7% of a typical AES Indiana residential bill. The article called rising utility costs a more pressing crisis than the ongoing property tax debate.

Indiana Capital Chronicle · 2025-08-22
Indiana electricity costs rise amid data center boom

A new Institute for Energy Economics and Financial Analysis (IEEFA) analysis highlighted a dramatic spike in capacity market prices set at auction by PJM, the regional electric grid operator covering Indiana, largely driven by surging demand from data centers. The report underscored how Indiana's utility rate environment is being shaped not just by utility capital investment but by broader wholesale electricity market dynamics. Consumer advocates warned the trend will further stress household budgets as other costs of living continue to rise.

Axios Indianapolis · 2025-08-07
Consumer advocates: Spike in electric bills shows Indiana energy plan isn't working

Citizens Action Coalition Program Director Ben Inskeep said 2025 marked the highest year-over-year jump in Indiana electric bills in two decades, arguing that a 2023 state law requiring the IURC to weigh five factors — including affordability — has failed to curb rate increases.
