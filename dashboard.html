Summary & Strategic Communications

Indiana is experiencing a historic energy affordability crisis, with average residential electric bills surging 17.5% statewide in a single year (2024–2025) — the largest year-over-year jump in at least two decades — adding roughly $28/month to household costs. Indiana's five largest investor-owned utilities (Duke Energy Indiana, AES Indiana, NIPSCO, CenterPoint Energy, and Indiana Michigan Power) have all recently received or are seeking major rate increases, driving record levels of consumer complaints to the IURC. Governor Braun responded by appointing a new, ratepayer-focused IURC majority and Utility Consumer Counselor, and signed landmark legislation (HEA 1002) in February 2026 that introduces performance-based ratemaking to tie utility returns to affordability benchmarks — though advocates warn the reforms do not go far enough for average ratepayers.

Messaging Recommendations Silverman & Smith

Low-income ratepayers and households receiving LIHEAP/EAP
You are not alone — Indiana's energy assistance programs (EAP/LIHEAP) offer moratorium protection from disconnection through March 15 and up to $625 in heating benefits; apply before April 20 each year and know your rights. Connect local, personal stories of energy insecurity to statewide data showing bills have risen more than 17% in one year to build public pressure on regulators and legislators.
Indiana legislators and IURC commissioners
Performance-based ratemaking under HEA 1002 is a meaningful first step, but structural protections — including robust low-income rate discounts, hard caps on rate increases, and strong enforcement of the moratorium — are needed to ensure accountability translates to real bill relief for the most vulnerable Hoosiers.
General Indiana ratepayers and media
Framing energy affordability as a 'kitchen-table' crisis — with average Hoosier households paying $28/month more than last year, and NIPSCO customers facing a 90%+ bill increase since 2016 — makes utility policy tangible and urgent, counteracting utility industry messaging that portrays rate increases as necessary for reliability and investment.
Utility company executives and regulatory parties
Hoosiers are watching and the regulatory landscape has changed: the IURC is actively investigating affordability, the OUCC is recommending rate reductions rather than increases, and new state law requires utilities to earn returns through demonstrated performance — making proactive low-income programs and billing transparency not just good public relations but regulatory necessity.

Recent Articles

Gov. Braun says new law will protect Indiana utility customers from rising bills

Governor Mike Braun signed House Enrolled Act 1002 (HEA 1002) into law at a ceremonial event at the Statehouse, describing the legislation as landmark reform that overhauls how Indiana regulates investor-owned utilities. The law introduces performance-based ratemaking, requiring utilities to meet measurable affordability and reliability benchmarks before earning their authorized return on equity. It also mandates more consistent and predictable billing, and requires utilities to offer low-income programs; Citizens Action Coalition said the law helps low-income Hoosiers but may not deliver meaningful relief for average ratepayers.

Newly unsealed IURC records revealed that NIPSCO overcharged more than 3,500 natural gas customers double their actual usage after installing new smart meters, a problem discovered in October 2024 that was not disclosed to state regulators for nearly a year. NIPSCO proposed refunding affected customers for only 12 months of overcharges per IURC rules, a position that Citizens Action Coalition called a violation of public trust. The disclosure came amid the IURC's ongoing affordability inquiry, adding to public scrutiny of the utility.

Utility Dive · 2025-09-08
Hoosiers' electric bills spike 17.5% in one year

A Citizens Action Coalition analysis of Indiana's five investor-owned electric utilities found that average residential bills rose 17.5%, or $28 per month, in the 12 months ending July 1, 2025 — the steepest year-over-year increase in at least 20 years. NIPSCO customers were hit hardest with a $50/month (26.7%) increase, while CenterPoint bills surged $44/month (25%). The report found that AES Indiana bills rose nearly $17/month (12%) and Duke Energy bills were up approximately $26/month (20%).
