Summary & Strategic Communications

Indiana is in the midst of a severe utility affordability crisis, with residential electric bills rising an average of 17.5% in one year—the sharpest increase in two decades—driven by major rate hikes approved for Duke Energy Indiana, NIPSCO, AES Indiana, and CenterPoint Energy. In response, the IURC launched a historic investigative inquiry in February 2026, summoning the state's five largest investor-owned utilities to explain rising costs, and Gov. Mike Braun signed HEA 1002 into law, introducing performance-based ratemaking and new low-income protections. Despite these reforms, over 122,000 Indiana households depend on LIHEAP/EAP assistance that faces federal funding uncertainty, and consumer advocates warn that recent legislative changes do not go far enough to provide immediate relief for struggling Hoosiers.

Messaging Recommendations Silverman & Smith

Low-income ratepayers and households enrolled in EAP/LIHEAP
Your utility bills should never force you to choose between heat and groceries—know your rights under Indiana's winter disconnection moratorium, new summer shutoff protections under HEA 1002, and automatic enrollment in levelized billing plans, and contact your local Community Action Agency if you need emergency help.
Indiana legislators and IURC commissioners
Hoosiers have experienced the steepest electric bill increases in 20 years, with some families paying $50 more per month than just a year ago—performance-based ratemaking (HEA 1002) is a first step, but trackers like TDSIC and CWIP must also be reformed, and the IURC must aggressively enforce its new affordability mandate rather than rubber-stamp future rate hike settlements.
Media and general public
Indiana's five investor-owned utilities requested billions in rate increases while reporting record profits, and the state's own consumer watchdog found that utilities like AES Indiana and NIPSCO had not demonstrated sufficient evidence for their requested hikes—Hoosiers deserve transparent billing, accountable regulators, and rates that are actually just and reasonable.
Corporate and industrial stakeholders and data center developers
Large new electricity loads from data centers and AI infrastructure must not be subsidized by residential ratepayers who are already struggling; Indiana's new NIPSCO GenCo model and HEA 1002 provisions offer a framework to ringfence large-load costs, and advocates will hold regulators accountable to that standard.

Recent Articles

Braun signs HEA 1002 to lower Indiana energy costs

Governor Mike Braun signed HEA 1002 into law, landmark legislation overhauling how Indiana regulates utilities by introducing performance-based ratemaking tied to measurable affordability and reliability benchmarks. The law replaces a model that had effectively guaranteed utility returns on equity regardless of customer outcomes. It also creates new low-income bill stabilization programs, a summer disconnection moratorium, and requirements for greater billing transparency.
