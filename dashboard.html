Summary & Strategic Communications

Indiana is experiencing a significant energy affordability crisis, with average residential electric bills rising 17.5% in a single year (2024–2025)—the most severe increase in at least two decades—driven by cascading rate approvals for all five major investor-owned utilities. In response, the Indiana General Assembly passed House Enrolled Act 1002 (signed February 2026), which introduces performance-based ratemaking and new low-income customer protections, while the IURC launched a landmark investigative inquiry into the 'big five' utilities in March 2026. Compounding affordability pressures are federal emergency orders keeping aging coal plants online at substantial cost to consumers, and rapidly growing energy demand from AI data centers that advocates warn risks shifting costs onto residential ratepayers.

Messaging Recommendations Silverman & Smith

Low-income ratepayers and households facing disconnection
Indiana's Energy Assistance Program (EAP/LIHEAP) can help you pay your heat and electric bills, and the new state law (HEA 1002) prohibits summer shutoffs for eligible low-income customers and requires utilities to offer assistance programs—know your rights and apply every fall when the portal reopens.
Indiana legislators and IURC commissioners
Indiana residential electric bills have climbed 28.9% since 2014, far outpacing inflation and peer states; the tools in HEA 1002 are a vital first step, but removing automatic cost-tracker mechanisms and strengthening IURC's authority to reject unjustified rate hikes are essential to making affordability reform durable.
General public and media
Every major Indiana electric utility raised rates significantly in 2024–2025, and data center growth now threatens to shift even more costs onto households; Hoosiers deserve transparent billing, genuine accountability from monopoly utilities, and assurance that large industrial customers pay their fair share of grid costs.
Community action agencies and social service providers
With the 2025–2026 EAP application cycle closed and bills at record highs, connect clients now with utility low-income assistance programs mandated under HEA 1002, and document the human cost of energy burden to strengthen IURC's investigative inquiry record ahead of the Fall 2026 EAP reopening.

Your electric bill is going to change. New law aims to stabilize utilities

Indiana's House Bill 1002 (now law) introduces several key changes to electric utility regulation, including requiring utilities to submit three-year rate plans, tying rates to performance metrics, mandating regular affordability data reporting, and requiring all residential customers to be enrolled in levelized billing (with an opt-out option). The law is intended to address pocketbook issues as energy demand and bills continue to rise. Some lawmakers said the measures do not go far enough to help everyday Hoosiers.
