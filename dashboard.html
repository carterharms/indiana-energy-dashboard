Summary & Strategic Communications

Indiana is experiencing a significant energy affordability crisis in 2025–2026, with electric bills for the state's five largest investor-owned utilities rising an average of 17.5% in a single year — with NIPSCO customers seeing the highest spike at 27% and CenterPoint customers at 25%. In response, Governor Braun signed HEA 1002 in February 2026 — requiring multi-year rate plans, new summer disconnection protections, and mandated low-income assistance programs — and the IURC launched an unprecedented 'investigative inquiry' and statewide listening tour. Despite these reforms, consumer advocates and the Office of Utility Consumer Counselor remain sharply critical of recent rate orders, including the IURC's June 2026 approval of a $71 million rate increase for AES Indiana and ongoing allegations that Duke Energy has over-collected $89 million from ratepayers.

Messaging Recommendations Silverman & Smith

Low-income ratepayers and households on fixed incomes
Rising utility bills are not inevitable — Indiana law now requires utilities to offer you assistance programs and protections from shutoffs on the hottest days. Know your rights: if you are enrolled in LIHEAP/EAP, your utility cannot disconnect you during extreme heat or winter months. Apply for the Energy Assistance Program when it reopens in fall 2026.
Indiana state legislators and IURC commissioners
HEA 1002 is a first step, but Hoosiers are still seeing rate increases that disproportionately burden residential customers over industrial users. Strong, consistent enforcement of affordability metrics in multi-year rate plans — and full accountability for utilities found to over-collect — is essential to make the new law meaningful.
General public and media
Indiana's utility bills have risen more sharply in the past year than at any point in two decades, and the pending AI data center build-out poses an additional threat to residential ratepayers who could be left subsidizing massive industrial energy loads. Community pressure — through IURC listening tours, public comment periods, and direct contact with lawmakers — is producing real results.
Community action agencies and social service providers
Indiana's LIHEAP-funded Energy Assistance Program provides a critical safety net for eligible households, but federal funding threats and program gaps mean that millions of Hoosiers — including those served by municipal utilities not covered by IURC jurisdiction — may fall through the cracks. Proactive enrollment outreach and coordination with utilities on payment plans is more urgent than ever.

WFYI · 2026-06-10
Indiana now blocks utility shutoffs on the hottest days — but only for some customers

A new Indiana law (HEA 1002) blocks privately and cooperatively owned electric utilities from disconnecting service within 48 hours of a forecast heat index of 95°F or higher — but only for customers enrolled in federal energy assistance (LIHEAP). Consumer advocates welcomed the protection as a meaningful first step while noting that a broader time-based rule would have covered more vulnerable Hoosiers.
