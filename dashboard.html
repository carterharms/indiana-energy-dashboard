Summary & Strategic Communications

Indiana is in the midst of a severe energy affordability crisis, with the five investor-owned electric utilities delivering the highest year-over-year residential bill increases in at least two decades — averaging 17.5% ($28/month) in 2025 alone, according to Citizens Action Coalition analysis. NIPSCO customers saw the sharpest spike (over 90% higher than 2016 levels), prompting the IURC to launch an unprecedented Investigative Inquiry on Energy Affordability in March 2026, hold statewide listening sessions, and the Indiana General Assembly to pass landmark reform legislation (HEA 1002) signed by Governor Braun in February 2026. Federal threats to LIHEAP funding and ongoing major rate cases from AES Indiana and Duke Energy compound the burden on low-income Hoosier households, even as the new law introduces multi-year rate plans, summer disconnection protections for LIHEAP-enrolled customers, and mandatory low-income assistance programs.

AES Indiana customers will see a temporary increase in bills this summer

AES Indiana customers face a temporary fuel adjustment charge of approximately $3.98/month (for a 1,000 kWh customer) on June–August 2026 bills due to winter fuel costs that exceeded forecasts, including costs associated with Winter Storm Fern in January 2026. The charge is separate from AES Indiana's pending base rate case (Cause No. 46258) and illustrates how tracker mechanisms can increase customer bills without a new Commission order. Citizens Action Coalition Executive Director Kerwin Olson noted the importance of tracking these additional charges beyond headline rate case figures.

WFYI Public Media · 2026-06-10
Indiana now blocks utility shutoffs on the hottest days — but only for some customers

Indiana's new law (HEA 1002) prohibits privately and cooperatively owned electric utilities from disconnecting a resident's service within 48 hours of a forecast heat index of 95°F or above — but only if the customer is enrolled in federal LIHEAP energy assistance. Critics including Indiana University professor David Konisky note the protection excludes low-income customers who qualify for assistance but have not successfully enrolled, leaving a significant gap in coverage. Indiana already prohibited winter shutoffs (Dec. 1–March 15) for LIHEAP-approved residents; the new law extends analogous protections to dangerous summer heat.

21Alive News (WPTA Fort Wayne) · 2026-03-18
Your electric bill is going to change. New law aims to stabilize utilities

Governor Mike Braun signed House Enrolled Act 1002 into law on February 26, 2026, reshaping how Indiana's electric utilities set rates and interact with low-income customers. The law requires utilities to file three-year Multi-Year Rate Plans, establishes performance incentive mechanisms tied to affordability and reliability, mandates low-income customer assistance programs, and creates the new summer heat disconnection moratorium for LIHEAP-enrolled customers. AARP Indiana called the bill 'a step in the right direction' while noting it preferred a broader summer moratorium.
