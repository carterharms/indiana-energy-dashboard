Summary & Strategic Communications

Indiana is experiencing an acute energy affordability crisis, with average residential electric bills surging approximately 17.5% in the 12-month period ending July 2025—the largest single-year jump in two decades—driven by a wave of rate increases from all five major investor-owned utilities. In response, Governor Mike Braun signed landmark legislation (HEA 1002) in February 2026 introducing performance-based ratemaking, low-income assistance requirements, and summer disconnection protections, while the newly reconstituted IURC under Chair Andy Zay launched a statewide Investigative Inquiry on Energy Affordability in March 2026, holding public hearings and a listening tour across ten Indiana communities. Compounding these pressures are a federal DOE emergency order forcing Indiana coal plants to remain open past their planned 2025 retirement dates, an IURC investigation into NIPSCO overcharging 3,500 gas customers double their actual usage, a pending AES Indiana rate case with a IURC final order deadline of June 24, 2026, and federal threats to eliminate LIHEAP funding—leaving more than 100,000 low-income Hoosier households who rely on the Energy Assistance Program in a precarious position.

Newly unsealed IURC records revealed NIPSCO discovered in October 2024 that faulty installation of new automated meter reading technology caused 3,542 customers to be billed double their actual gas usage, but the utility did not notify state regulators for nearly a year—until November 2025. Gov. Braun signs House Bill 1002 to tackle Indiana's energy affordability crisis

Governor Mike Braun signed HEA 1002 into law on February 26, 2026, a sweeping energy bill backed with bipartisan support. The law introduces performance-based ratemaking tied to customer affordability metrics, requires utilities to offer low-income assistance programs by July 1, 2026, mandates levelized billing for EAP-enrolled households, and creates a summer disconnection moratorium on days when the heat index is forecast to reach 95°F or higher.
