Summary & Strategic Communications

Indiana is experiencing its most severe electric utility affordability crisis in at least two decades. Between mid-2024 and mid-2025, statewide residential electric bills rose an average of 17.5% — roughly $28 per month — with NIPSCO customers in northwest Indiana seeing increases approaching $50 per month. A wave of major rate cases by Duke Energy Indiana, NIPSCO, AES Indiana, and CenterPoint Energy was approved or decided by the IURC during 2025–2026, adding further pressure on households; a landmark June 2026 IURC vote to grant AES Indiana an additional $71 million triggered Governor Braun to replace the IURC chairman and call for rehearing, while the legislature passed HEA 1002 in February 2026 — Indiana's first major electric utility affordability law in over a decade — introducing summer shutoff protections, multi-year rate plans with performance incentives, and automatic levelized billing for low-income households.

Messaging Recommendations Silverman & Smith

Low-income ratepayers and EAP-enrolled households
You have new rights starting this summer: Indiana's new law (HEA 1002) now protects you from electric shutoffs on days forecast to reach a 95-degree heat index, and automatically enrolls you in a levelized billing plan after June 30, 2026 — call your utility or the OUCC at 1-888-441-2494 to confirm your enrollment and ask about bill assistance through the Energy Assistance Program.
Indiana state legislators and the Governor's office
HEA 1002 was a critical first step, but Indiana residential customers still face structural barriers: unelected regulators who are not confirmed by the legislature, trackers that bypass full rate cases, and utilities that repeatedly seek returns on equity well above what the OUCC recommends — further statutory reform capping tracker revenue recovery and requiring IURC commissioner confirmation is needed to make affordability durable.
General public / media
Indiana's five investor-owned utilities have secured rate increases totaling hundreds of millions of dollars since 2024, while the Indiana LIHEAP/EAP program served only about 122,000 households in FY2024 — a fraction of those struggling to pay bills — making the gap between utility profits and household energy burdens a defining kitchen-table issue for Hoosiers heading into the 2026 election cycle.
Community action agencies and faith-based organizations
Partner with the OUCC and Citizens Action Coalition to help clients understand and act on new HEA 1002 protections: automatic levelized billing, the new summer shutoff moratorium, and the right to petition for rehearing of utility rate orders — and mobilize client voices in IURC listening sessions and public comment processes, which demonstrably influence commission decisions.

WFYI Public Media · 2026-06-10
Indiana now blocks utility shutoffs on the hottest days — but only for some customers

Indiana's new HEA 1002 law prohibits privately and cooperatively owned electric utilities from disconnecting residential service within 48 hours of a forecast heat index of 95°F or higher — but only for customers enrolled in the federal energy assistance program. Consumer advocates celebrated the new protection as a long-sought safeguard, especially given rising heat-related health risks, but noted it leaves non-EAP-enrolled vulnerable customers without coverage.

How Indy's rising electricity bills stack up

AES Indiana customers in central Indiana faced multiple converging rate pressures in 2026: a base rate case requesting 13.5% increases in two phases, previously approved tracker charges adding roughly 6% more, and a summer fuel adjustment surcharge. The OUCC had recommended a rate reduction rather than an increase. Residential customers in Indianapolis have seen their bills climb sharply compared to state and national averages.

Your electric bill is going to change. New law aims to stabilize utilities

Indiana's HEA 1002, signed by Gov. Braun on February 26, 2026, introduces multi-year rate plans with performance-based incentives, a summer shutoff moratorium tied to heat advisories, automatic levelized billing for low-income EAP-enrolled customers after June 30, 2026, and new transparency requirements for utility bills. The law is the first major electric utility affordability reform in Indiana in over a decade. Consumer advocates called it a 'good start' while pushing for stronger measures.
