Summary & Strategic Communications

Indiana is experiencing a severe energy affordability crisis, with statewide average residential electric bills spiking 17.5% in a single year (2024–2025) — the largest year-over-year jump since at least 2005 — driven by a wave of large rate increases across all five major investor-owned utilities. In response, Gov. Mike Braun signed landmark utility reform legislation (HEA 1002) in April 2026 introducing performance-based ratemaking, and the IURC launched an unprecedented Investigative Inquiry on Energy Affordability in March 2026, publicly questioning all five major utilities about rising bills. Low-income Hoosiers face particular pressure as LIHEAP faces ongoing federal threats to its funding, even as Indiana's winter disconnection moratorium and new summer shut-off protections under HEA 1002 provide some protection.

Messaging Recommendations Silverman & Smith

Low-income ratepayers and households on fixed incomes
You have legal protections — Indiana's winter disconnection moratorium shields LIHEAP-eligible customers from shutoff Dec. 1 through March 15, and new 2026 law adds summer heat-index protections; contact your local Community Action Agency to apply for energy assistance before the fall 2026 application window opens.
Indiana state legislators and IURC commissioners
Performance-based ratemaking under HEA 1002 is a critical first step, but robust enforcement and mandatory low-income affordability programs are needed now — Indiana's five major utilities collectively raised residential bills an average of 17.5% in one year, and roughly one in five Hoosiers are not covered by current regulatory protections.
General ratepayers and media
Indiana's electricity bills have risen faster than almost any state in the country in recent years — NIPSCO customers are paying 90% more than in 2016, and CenterPoint customers average $221/month — framing these increases as a systemic regulatory failure, not an inevitable market outcome, builds public support for structural reform.
Utility executives and IURC
Transparency and accountability are now legal requirements: HEA 1002 mandates Multi-Year Rate Plans with customer affordability metrics tied to CPI, and utilities that fail to meet performance benchmarks risk reduced returns on equity — advocates should monitor and publicize compliance closely.

Evansville City Council calls for pause on CenterPoint Energy rate increases

The Evansville City Council passed a resolution 6-0 urging the IURC to pause all electricity rate hikes by CenterPoint Energy in the city. The action came less than a week after the IURC held an energy affordability listening session in Evansville as part of its statewide inquiry. Braun signs HEA 1002 to lower Indiana energy costs

Gov. Mike Braun signed House Enrolled Act 1002 into law on April 7, 2026, overhauling Indiana's utility regulatory framework by introducing performance-based ratemaking that requires utilities to meet measurable benchmarks for affordability and reliability. Under the new law, returns on equity are no longer guaranteed, and utilities must demonstrate accountability to customers. The law also mandates low-income customer assistance programs, predictable billing plans, and a summer disconnection moratorium during extreme heat events.

'Big five' Indiana utilities defend rates in regulator questioning

The IURC held a landmark five-hour Investigative Inquiry on Energy Affordability on March 24, 2026, publicly questioning the leaders of Indiana's five largest investor-owned electric utilities about rising bills. According to the IURC's own survey data, NIPSCO customers averaged $234/month (up 27%) and CenterPoint customers averaged $221/month (up 25%) as of July 2025. Utility executives largely attributed high bills to customer usage and infrastructure investment costs, while regulators and lawmakers expressed frustration with the lack of concrete solutions offered.

NIPSCO overcharged 3,500 natural gas customers, newly unsealed records show

Newly unsealed records revealed NIPSCO admitted to overcharging approximately 3,542 natural gas customers — billing them double their actual usage — due to incorrectly installed mechanical meter indexes that were not rotating at the correct drive rate. NIPSCO did not initially inform state regulators about the full extent of the problem. The company said affected customers would receive refunds for 12 months of overcharges, and a formal IURC evidentiary hearing on the matter is scheduled for July 2, 2026.

Indiana Capital Chronicle · 2025-10-31
Indiana's Energy Assistance Program now open: What you need to know

Indiana's Energy Assistance Program (EAP/LIHEAP) opened for the 2025–2026 application season on October 1, 2025, accepting applications through April 20, 2026. The program, funded by federal LIHEAP dollars, provides a one-time annual benefit toward heating and electric bills for eligible low-income households. Indiana received $87.1 million in FY2026 LIHEAP federal funding, though the program faces potential elimination in future federal budget cycles under the Trump administration's FY2027 proposals.
